A Minimal Sample Paper for Treaties Smoke Tests

Authors: A. Researcher, B. Collaborator
Year: 2024

Abstract
We investigate whether moderate sleep restriction (5 hours/night for 7 days) impairs working memory
in healthy adults. In a within-subjects design with 32 participants, we found a 14% drop in n-back
accuracy (p = 0.003) and a corresponding rise in reaction-time variability. Effects partially recovered
after a single 9-hour recovery night.

1. Problem
Sleep loss is widely believed to impair cognition, but quantifying the effect on working memory in
naturalistic mild-restriction conditions remains contested.

2. Method
Within-subjects crossover. Each participant served as their own control. Working memory was measured
with a 2-back letter task. Sleep was monitored with wrist actigraphy and self-report diaries. The
study was preregistered at OSF.

3. Variables
Independent variable: nightly sleep duration (5h vs 8h target).
Dependent variables: n-back accuracy (%), n-back reaction time (ms), reaction-time variability (CV).

4. Mechanism
We hypothesize that sleep restriction degrades prefrontal-cortex-mediated maintenance of task sets,
reducing both accuracy and consistency.

5. Evidence
Mean accuracy fell from 89.2% (SD 4.1) at baseline to 76.5% (SD 6.2) after restriction (paired t(31) =
3.41, p = 0.003). Reaction-time variability (CV) rose by 22% (p = 0.011). Effect sizes were medium
(Cohen's d = 0.62 for accuracy).

6. Limitations
Small sample, single university subject pool, no objective polysomnography, recovery follow-up was
limited to 24 hours. Generalisation to clinical sleep disorders is not warranted.

7. Implications
Mild chronic sleep restriction measurably impairs working memory in healthy adults at effect sizes
relevant for safety-critical work. Replication with polysomnography is needed.
